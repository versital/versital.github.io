# The Sunk Cost Fallacy: Why We Struggle to Let Go

Have you ever sat through a terrible movie just because you paid for the ticket? Or stayed in a miserable relationship because you've "already put five years into it"? If so, you've fallen victim to the **Sunk Cost Fallacy**.

The Sunk Cost Fallacy is a cognitive bias that leads us to continue investing in a losing proposition because of the resources we have already spent, even when the current costs outweigh the future benefits.

## 1. What is a "Sunk Cost"?
In economics, a **sunk cost** is money, time, or effort that has already been spent and cannot be recovered. 

The fundamental rule of rational decision-making is that **sunk costs should be ignored**. When deciding whether to continue an action, you should only look at the *future* costs and *future* benefits. The past is gone; it cannot be "bought back" by spending more.

## 2. How the Fallacy Works
The fallacy occurs when we confuse "investment" with "value." We feel that by quitting, we are "wasting" everything we've put in so far. 

In reality, the resources are *already* wasted. By continuing to invest in a failing project, you aren't recovering your past loss—you are simply adding a *new* loss to it.

### The Logic Trap:
- **Rational Thought:** "I've spent $100 on this car repair, but the car is still broken and will cost another $1,000 to fix. It's not worth it. I'll stop now."
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy:** "I've already spent $100 on this car repair! I can't stop now, or that $100 will have been for nothing. I have to keep going until it's fixed."

## 3. Real-World Examples
The Sunk Cost Fallacy appears in almost every area of human life:

### In Business and Government
- **The Concorde Fallacy:** The British and French governments continued to fund the Concorde supersonic jet long after it was clear the project was not economically viable, simply because they had already invested so much money.
- **Software Projects:** A company spends two years and millions of dollars on a new internal system. Halfway through, they realize the technology is obsolete. Instead of pivoting, they spend another two years finishing it because they "can't let the first two years go to waste."

### In Personal Life
- **Education:** Staying in a degree program you hate because you've already finished two years of it.
- **Reading:** Forcing yourself to finish a boring book because you've already read 200 pages.
- **Gaming:** Playing a tedious game just because you spent $60 on it.

## 4. How to Overcome the Fallacy
The next time you feel the urge to "push through" a failing project, ask yourself these three questions:

1. **The Clean Slate Test:** "If I walked into this situation today for the first time, with zero previous investment, would I choose to start this project right now?"
2. **The Future Cost Test:** "Regardless of what I've spent, will the *future* investment of time/money result in a benefit that exceeds the cost?"
3. **The Opportunity Cost Test:** "What *else* could I be doing with my time and money if I stop doing this right now?"

## Summary: Cut Your Losses
The most successful people are not those who never fail, but those who know when to quit. Learning to "cut your losses" is a superpower. It frees up your most valuable resources—time and attention—to be invested in things that actually work.
