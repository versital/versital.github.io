# Survivorship Bias: The Danger of Only Looking at the Winners

Why do we believe that "they don't make things like they used to"? Why do we think that dropping out of college is a viable path to success because Bill Gates and Mark Zuckerberg did it? Why do we believe that a particular investment strategy is a "sure thing" after reading a book written by a millionaire?

The answer is **Survivorship Bias**.

## 1. What is Survivorship Bias?
Survivorship Bias is a logical error that occurs when we focus on the people, things, or processes that "survived" a selection process and overlook those that did not, typically because they are no longer visible.

By focusing only on the "survivors," we draw overly optimistic conclusions and create a distorted view of reality. We see the success as a result of a specific set of actions, while ignoring the thousands of others who took the exact same actions but failed.

## 2. The Classic Example: Abraham Wald and the WWII Bombers
The most famous illustration of survivorship bias comes from World War II. The US military wanted to armor their bombers to reduce losses. They examined the bombers returning from missions and found that the bullet holes were concentrated on the fuselage and the wings.

**The Intuitive (Wrong) Conclusion:** "We should add armor to the fuselage and wings because that's where the planes are getting hit the most."

**The Correct Conclusion (Abraham Wald):** Mathematician Abraham Wald realized that the military was only looking at the *survivors*. The planes that were hit in the fuselage and wings were able to fly home. The planes that were hit in the **engines** and the **cockpit** didn't survive to be analyzed—they crashed.

Wald argued that the armor should be placed where the bullet holes *weren't*, because those were the fatal spots.

## 3. Common Modern Traps

### The "Dropout Millionaire" Myth
We hear stories about Steve Jobs, Bill Gates, and Mark Zuckerberg dropping out of college to start billion-dollar companies. This creates a narrative that higher education can be a hindrance to extreme success.
- **The Survivors:** The handful of geniuses who dropped out and won.
- **The Invisible Failures:** The millions of people who dropped out of college and struggled to find stable employment.
- **The Lesson:** Dropping out didn't *cause* their success; their exceptional skill and timing did. For the vast majority, the "map" of success still includes a degree.

### The "Vintage Quality" Fallacy
People often look at a 100-year-old house or a 50-year-old watch and conclude that "things were built better in the past."
- **The Survivors:** The few exceptionally well-made items that happened to survive.
- **The Invisible Failures:** The millions of poorly made houses and watches from 1920 that rotted, broke, and were thrown in the trash decades ago.
- **The Lesson:** You aren't seeing "average" quality from the past; you are seeing the *top 1%* of quality from the past.

### The "Expert Investor" Guide
Many investment books are written by people who made millions in a specific market. They describe the "secrets" to their success.
- **The Survivors:** The investor who happened to be on the right side of a volatile market.
- **The Invisible Failures:** The thousands of people who used the exact same "secrets" but were wiped out by a market crash.
- **The Lesson:** Success in high-risk environments often involves a degree of luck. Mimicking the actions of a survivor without understanding the role of luck is a recipe for disaster.

## 4. How to Avoid Survivorship Bias
To make better decisions, you must actively look for the "missing" data.

1. **Ask "Who is NOT in this sample?":** Whenever you see a success story, ask yourself: "How many people tried this and failed?"
2. **Study the Failures:** If you want to know how to succeed, don't just study the winners. Study the losers. Finding out why people fail is often more informative than finding out why one person succeeded.
3. **Beware of Anecdotes:** A single success story (an anecdote) is not a data point; it's a survivor. Look for systemic data and statistical averages rather than exceptional examples.
4. **Question the Narrative:** If a strategy is presented as "proven" because of a few high-profile examples, be skeptical.

## Summary: Look for the Holes
Survivorship bias blinds us by highlighting the trophies and hiding the graveyards. To see the world clearly, you have to stop looking at the planes that made it home and start wondering about the ones that didn't.
