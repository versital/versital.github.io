# The Law of Triviality: Why We Waste Time on "Bike Shedding"

Imagine a corporate committee meeting to discuss three projects:
1. A £10 million nuclear power plant.
2. A £350 bicycle shed.
3. A £21 annual coffee budget.

Logically, the committee should spend the vast majority of its time debating the nuclear plant—the most expensive and complex project. Instead, they spend five minutes approving the power plant (because it's too complex for most of them to understand) and two hours arguing about what color to paint the bicycle shed.

This is the **Law of Triviality**, more commonly known as **Bike Shedding**.

## 1. What is the Law of Triviality?
The Law of Triviality states that organizations give disproportionate weight to trivial issues. The time spent on any item of an agenda is usually in inverse proportion to the amount of money or complexity involved.

The "Bicycle Shed" is a metaphor for any minor detail that is easy for everyone to understand and have an opinion on. Because it's easy to grasp, everyone feels qualified to contribute, leading to endless debate.

## 2. Why Does Bike Shedding Happen?
Bike shedding occurs because of three primary psychological drivers:

### The Comfort of the Familiar
Complexity is intimidating. Most people are uncomfortable admitting they don't understand the technical details of a nuclear reactor or a complex software architecture. To avoid feeling ignorant, they steer the conversation toward something they *do* understand—like the color of the paint or the layout of the office.

### The Need to Feel Valued
In a meeting, people often feel a subconscious need to contribute to be seen as "productive" or "engaged." If the main topic is too complex for them to contribute meaningfully, they will find a trivial detail to comment on just to ensure their voice is heard.

### The Illusion of Progress
Solving a trivial problem feels like a "win." Spending an hour arguing about a logo and finally reaching a decision provides a hit of dopamine and a sense of accomplishment, even though the actual value added to the company is near zero.

## 3. Real-World Examples

### In Software Development
A team spends three days debating the naming convention for a specific set of variables (the bike shed) while the actual system architecture—which will determine if the app scales or crashes under load—is approved in a ten-minute meeting without any real scrutiny.

### In Product Design
A company spends weeks arguing over the exact placement of a "Sign Up" button (a triviality) while ignoring the fact that the core value proposition of the product is unclear to the user (the nuclear plant).

### In Personal Life
Spending two hours researching the "perfect" $20 water bottle while ignoring the fact that you are $10,000 in high-interest credit card debt.

## 4. How to Prevent Bike Shedding
To stop your meetings and projects from devolving into bike shedding, implement these strategies:

### 1. Limit the Scope of Debate
Explicitly define which decisions are "trivial" and which are "critical." For trivial decisions, appoint a single "Decision Owner" who has the final say, rather than seeking a group consensus.

### 2. Use "Silent" Input
Instead of an open debate, have people submit their thoughts on the complex issues in writing first. This prevents the "loudest voice" from dominating the conversation and forces people to engage with the actual complexity of the problem.

### 3. Front-Load the Complexity
Start meetings with the most complex, high-stakes items first. Force the group to wrestle with the "nuclear plant" while they still have the mental energy to do so, rather than leaving it for the end when everyone is tired and looking for an easy win.

### 4. Call it Out
When you notice a meeting spiraling into a debate about a triviality, use the term. *"I think we're bike-shedding the color of the button right now. Let's move back to the core architecture."*

## Summary: Focus on the Nuclear Plant
The Law of Triviality is a reminder that "activity" is not the same as "productivity." Just because a meeting was loud and a decision was reached doesn't mean the most important work was done. Stop painting the bike shed and start building the reactor.
