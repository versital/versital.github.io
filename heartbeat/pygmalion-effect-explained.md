# The Pygmalion and Golem Effects: The Power of Expectation

We often think of performance as a result of skill, effort, and intelligence. But there is a hidden, powerful variable that can either accelerate a person's growth or stifle it completely: **the expectations of others.**

This is captured in two opposing psychological phenomena: the **Pygmalion Effect** and the the **Golem Effect**.

## 1. The Pygmalion Effect: The Upward Spiral
The Pygmalion Effect (also known as the Rosenthal Effect) is a psychological phenomenon where higher expectations lead to an increase in performance.

### How it Works:
It is a self-fulfilling prophecy that functions as a feedback loop:
1. **Expectation:** A leader or teacher believes that a specific individual has high potential.
2. **Behavior:** Because of this belief, the leader treats the individual differently—providing more challenging tasks, more constructive feedback, and more genuine encouragement.
3. **Internalization:** The individual senses this belief and begins to view themselves as more capable. Their confidence and motivation increase.
4. **Performance:** The individual works harder and more effectively, eventually meeting or exceeding the original high expectations.

**The Result:** The original belief, even if it was arbitrary or unfounded, becomes a reality.

## 2. The Golem Effect: The Downward Spiral
The Golem Effect is the dark mirror of the Pygmalion Effect. It occurs when low expectations lead to a decrease in performance.

### How it Works:
1. **Expectation:** A leader or teacher believes an individual is incompetent or lacks potential.
2. **Behavior:** The leader provides less support, gives simpler (and boring) tasks, and offers less positive reinforcement. They may also be more critical of small mistakes.
3. **Internalization:** The individual senses the lack of trust and belief. Their self-esteem drops, and they stop trying to excel because they feel it is pointless.
4. **Performance:** The individual's performance declines, confirming the leader's original (negative) belief.

**The Result:** The individual is "trapped" by the low expectations of others, leading to a waste of potential and a toxic environment.

## 3. Real-World Applications

### In Education
In a famous study by Rosenthal and Jacobson, teachers were told that certain students were "academic bloomers" who would show great improvement. These students were chosen **at random**. However, because the teachers expected more from them, they subconsciously gave those students more attention and support. By the end of the year, the "bloomers" actually showed significantly higher IQ gains than their peers.

### In the Workplace
A manager who views a new hire as a "superstar" will often delegate more interesting and high-impact projects to them. This gives the new hire more experience and visibility, which in turn makes them more likely to actually become a superstar. Conversely, the employee labeled as "average" is given routine work, ensuring they stay average.

### In Personal Relationships
The expectations we hold for our partners or children often shape their behavior. When we focus on a child's strengths and expect them to be resilient, they often develop that resilience. When we focus on their flaws and expect them to fail, we may accidentally be training them for that failure.

## 4. How to Use These Effects Positively
You can use the Pygmalion Effect to unlock potential in yourself and others:

- **Assume Potential:** Treat people as if they are already capable of the growth you want to see in them.
- **Provide "High-Expectation" Support:** Don't just say "I believe in you." Provide the specific challenges and the support necessary to meet those high expectations.
- **Audit Your Biases:** Be aware of your own "Golem" tendencies. Ask yourself: *"Am I giving this person less opportunity because they lack skill, or because I've already decided they aren't capable?"*
- **The Self-Pygmalion Effect:** Set high, but attainable, expectations for yourself. View your challenges not as obstacles, but as evidence of your expanding capability.

## Summary: You are a Mirror
The Pygmalion and Golem effects remind us that we don't just observe the people around us—we help create them. Your expectations act as a mirror; if you reflect brilliance and potential, people often rise to meet it. If you reflect doubt and limitation, they often sink to meet it.
